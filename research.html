For my final year dissertation, I explored why the same ad can win over one person and completely miss another — and what that means for how brands talk to people.

01

The Dissertation

BMT440 · Abertay University · 2024

The Effect of Self-Monitoring on Purchase Intentions Towards Advertising Self-Presentation Styles

Module BMT440 — Marketing Dissertation
Programme BA (Hons) Marketing & Business
Word Count 9,846 words
Ethics Ref EMS8342 — Full Approval
Supervisors Prof. Andreea Oniga · Prof. G. Forgues-Puccio

Abstract

Some people are drawn to bold, aspirational ads — the kind that sell a lifestyle. Others switch off the moment something feels like it's overselling. I wanted to understand why, and whether personality could help explain it.

The research focused on a trait called self-monitoring — how much someone adjusts their behaviour based on social cues. Using a real experiment with 152 participants, I tested whether high and low self-monitors respond differently to two contrasting ad styles: one boastful, one refreshingly honest. The results showed they do — and that personality is a more powerful driver of purchase intent than the ad itself.

02

Hypotheses & Results

H1 — Confirmed

Personality shapes how people respond to ads — not just the ad itself

The experiment confirmed that self-monitoring level significantly changed how people reacted to the same product depending on how it was advertised. Personality was the deciding factor, not the message alone.

Supported · significant interaction
H1a — Inconclusive

People who care about image gravitate toward aspirational advertising

High self-monitors did lean toward the bolder, more aspirational ad — but not strongly enough to be conclusive. It pointed in the right direction, which opens the door for future research with branded products.

Directional · not significant
H1b — Confirmed

Authentic, honest ads land harder with people who value substance over style

Low self-monitors — those less driven by social image — responded significantly more to the honest, straightforward ad. The gap in purchase intent between the two ad styles was striking and consistent.

Supported · clear difference found
03

Methodology

⬡

How It Was Set Up

I created two versions of a shampoo ad — one bold and aspirational, the other honest and understated. Participants were randomly shown one version and asked how likely they were to buy. This meant responses weren't influenced by seeing both, keeping the results clean.

◎

Who Took Part

158 people responded via Instagram and LinkedIn. After quality checks, 152 valid responses were analysed. The group was deliberately varied in age and background to avoid a skewed picture. Each participant was then classified as a high or low self-monitor based on an established personality scale.

152 Participants
79 Image-driven
73 Authenticity-driven
▣

What Was Measured

Purchase intent was measured using four statements rated on a scale from strongly disagree to strongly agree. Personality type was measured using the Snyder & Gangestad Self-Monitoring Scale — 18 true/false questions that reveal how much someone shapes their behaviour around social expectations.

△

What the Data Showed

The numbers were crunched in SPSS, comparing how strongly purchase intent differed across personality types and ad styles. The key test looked for an interaction — whether one type of person responded differently to one type of ad — and it found one.

04

Key Findings

01

Who your audience is matters more than what you say. The biggest takeaway was that the same ad performed very differently depending on the person's personality type. The message only worked when it matched who was reading it.

Key finding
02

Honest advertising isn't just a trend — it genuinely converts. People who value authenticity over image were significantly more likely to want to buy a product when it was advertised honestly and without hype. The difference in purchase intent was meaningful and consistent.

Confirmed
03

Aspirational ads need more than bold copy. Image-conscious consumers pointed toward the bolder ad style, but weren't fully convinced — likely because the product had no brand attached. Brand identity probably plays a bigger role here than the words alone.

Needs more research
04

One size really doesn't fit all. Neither ad style won outright on its own. The data showed that without thinking about who you're speaking to, even a well-crafted campaign can fall flat — which is the core argument for audience-first marketing.

Confirmed
05

Personality is a stable, underused targeting lens. Unlike mood or context, personality doesn't change much. That makes it a reliable signal for brands wanting to match their tone of voice to the right audience — something this research points to as genuinely worth exploring further.
